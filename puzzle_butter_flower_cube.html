The Butter Flower Cube

As of this writing, I've only solved this once, but I'm going to do a write-up of it anyway. It's a fun puzzle. I'm surprised at how both easy and hard it was. Easy, because it didn't take long at all to find the necessary sequences, but also a bit hard, because the setups needed in the final step are difficult (but sometimes they get easier with practice.) Anyhow, I wasn't able to always make reductions in the final step. Rather, sometimes I had to turn one problem into another, or even back-track a bit. Again, with practice, maybe the need for such things will no longer occur.

There are 3 types of faces: large pedal, small pedal, and diamond. (Should be reasonably clear which I mean here.) And there is just one type of edge piece. There are only two types of rotations: corner and edge. (The faces don't turn.)

Step 1

Solve the diamonds first. This is a trivial matter of turning the edges.

Step 2

Solve the edges next. There is a trivial tri-cycle for this. It's a commutator between and edge and corner.

Step 3

Solve the large pedals. This is a trivial tri-cycle (or commutator) between adjacent edges.

Step 4

Solve the small pedals. This is the trickiest part. My stratagy was to perform the tri-cycle in Step 3, but with a setup ensure that all 3 large pedals involved are the same color. This will tri-cycle 3 small pedals while leaving the rest of the cube invarient. You then have to find setups for this stratagy to tri-cycle the desired triples of small pedals. Where it gets tricky is in realizing that sometimes you have to change orbits in the setup sequence. Doing so requires a special move only available on the curvy-copter, which is a sub-group (or factor group?) of this cube.

I suppose there might be a solve method where you first reduce the dube to a curvy-copter, and then solve as a curvy-copter. I'll have to give that a try. My guess is that once reduced to a curvy-copter, you might encounter cases that can't happen on a normal curvy-copter. This classic example, of course, is the 4x4 Rubic's Cube. Once reduced to a 3x3, it's not necessarily a true 3x3, because you may encounter cases that can't happen on a normal 3x3, such as having a single edge flipped.
